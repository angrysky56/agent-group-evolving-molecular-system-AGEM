> **Type:** test fixture + memory seed. **Not a skill.**
> **Purpose:** same spine-stress as the AGEM-lineage seed (compaction → lumpability → v2

## How to run it

Identical protocol to the AGEM-lineage seed: accumulate past your escalation thresholds to force
a compaction, check each Answer-Key pair against the resulting `SummaryNode`, then save → reload
to verify entry IDs survive and embeddings aren't recomputed. A collapsed distinction that the
auditor misses is a sensitivity finding, not a corpus failure.

## Answer key — distinctions the auditor must protect

1. **Hard problem** (why experience exists at all) ≠ **easy problems** (functions/mechanisms).
2. **Phenomenal consciousness** (what-it's-like) ≠ **access consciousness** (availability for report/reasoning).
3. **IIT's Φ** (intrinsic integrated cause-effect) ≠ **Global Workspace broadcast** (information made widely available).
4. **Higher-Order Thought** (the meta-state is thought-like) ≠ **Higher-Order Perception** (it is perception-like).
5. **Prediction error** (mismatch signal) ≠ **precision** (confidence/gain weighting on that signal).
6. **Qualia** (felt quality) ≠ **intentionality** (aboutness/directedness).
7. **Type physicalism** (mental type = physical type) ≠ **functionalism** (mental = causal role, multiply realizable).
8. **Epiphenomenalism** (mental is caused but causally inert) ≠ **interactionist dualism** (mental causes physical).
9. **Explanatory gap** (epistemic) ≠ **conceivability/zombie argument** (modal-metaphysical).
10. **Multiple realizability** (a kind can be realized in many substrates) ≠ **substrate independence** (a thesis about what consciousness *requires*).

## Corpus

### 1. The hard problem and the easy problems are different kinds of question

David Chalmers' distinction is categorical, not a matter of difficulty ranking. The **easy
problems** ask how the brain performs functions: discriminating stimuli, integrating
information, reporting states, controlling behavior. They are "easy" only in that we know what a
solution would *look like* — a mechanism. The **hard problem** asks why the performance of any
of those functions is accompanied by subjective experience at all — why it is *like something*
to be the system rather than all going on "in the dark." Solving every easy problem would yield
a complete functional account and still, the argument goes, leave the hard problem untouched.
Collapsing them — "consciousness is hard but we're making progress on it" — quietly converts the
hard problem into a hard *easy* problem, which is exactly the move Chalmers says begs the
question.

### 2. Phenomenal vs access consciousness

Ned Block separates two things the word "conscious" runs together. **Phenomenal consciousness**
(P-consciousness) is experiential character — the redness of red, the ache of an ache, the
what-it's-likeness. **Access consciousness** (A-consciousness) is functional availability: a
state is A-conscious if its content is poised for use in reasoning, reporting, and the rational
control of action. The two *usually* co-occur, which is why they get fused, but Block argues
they can in principle dissociate — phenomenal states not globally accessed, or access without
(or with mismatched) phenomenal character. The distinction is load-bearing: most scientific
theories of consciousness are, on inspection, theories of *access*, and whether they thereby
explain *phenomenality* is precisely what is in dispute.

### 3. Φ is intrinsic; broadcast is relational

Integrated Information Theory and Global Workspace Theory are often filed together as
"information theories of consciousness," but they locate consciousness in opposite places. IIT's
**Φ** measures *intrinsic* integrated cause-effect power — how much a system's current state
constrains its own past and future *from its own perspective*, irreducible to its parts;
consciousness, for IIT, just *is* maximally irreducible integrated information, substrate's
intrinsic structure and all. Global Workspace's **broadcast** is *relational* and functional:
a content becomes conscious when it wins competition for a global workspace and is made widely
available to many specialized subsystems. One says "consciousness is intrinsic integration
whether or not anything is broadcast"; the other says "consciousness is broadcast availability."
A summary fusing them into "consciousness as information integration" erases a real fight about
whether the relevant property is intrinsic or relational.

### 4. Higher-order: thought vs perception

Higher-order theories agree that a mental state is conscious in virtue of being the target of a
*further* mental state directed at it — but they split on the nature of that further state.
**Higher-Order Thought (HOT)** theories (Rosenthal) hold the meta-state is *thought-like*:
a conceptual, assertoric representation that "I am in state M." **Higher-Order Perception**
(or "inner sense") theories hold it is *perception-like*: a quasi-sensory monitoring of one's
own mind, more like an internal scanner than a judgment. The difference drives different
predictions about misrepresentation, conceptual requirements for consciousness, and what
animals or infants lacking rich concepts can experience. "Consciousness requires a higher-order
state" is the shared core; whether that state is a thought or a perception is the dividing line
a summary must not sand off.

### 5. Prediction error is the signal; precision is the volume knob

In predictive processing, the cortex is cast as a hierarchical generative model that predicts
its inputs and propagates **prediction error** — the mismatch between predicted and actual
signals — upward to revise the model. But not all errors are treated equally: **precision** is
the *expected reliability* (inverse variance) assigned to a given error stream, implemented as a
gain that amplifies or suppresses it. Prediction error is *what* is wrong; precision is *how
much to care*. Attention, on this view, just is the optimization of precision. Conflating them —
"the brain minimizes prediction error" without the precision-weighting story — loses the entire
account of attention, and of pathologies (e.g. aberrant precision in hallucination) that turn on
mis-set gain rather than mis-computed error.

### 6. Qualia and intentionality are two features, not one

A mental state can have **qualia** — felt qualitative character, the way an experience feels from
the inside — and it can have **intentionality** — aboutness, being *directed at* or *of*
something. A pain has vivid qualia and arguably thin intentionality; an abstract belief that
2+2=4 has rich intentionality and arguably no qualia. Whether the two ultimately reduce to one
another is a live debate (representationalists try to explain qualia *via* intentional content;
others deny it), but that debate only exists *because the distinction is real to begin with*.
A summary that treats "the felt, meaningful character of experience" as one undivided property
prejudges representationalism and erases the explananda the debate is about.

### 7. Type physicalism vs functionalism

Both are physicalist-friendly, yet they make incompatible claims about what mental kinds *are*.
**Type physicalism** (the identity theory) says each mental type is identical to a specific
physical type — pain *is* C-fiber firing, full stop. **Functionalism** says a mental kind is
defined by its *causal role* — its pattern of relations among inputs, outputs, and other states
— so anything that plays the pain-role *is* pain, whatever its physical makeup. The crux is
**multiple realizability**: if octopi, silicon, or martians could feel pain via utterly
different hardware, then pain cannot be identical to any one physical type, and functionalism
wins over type identity. Folding them into "the mind is physical" hides that they disagree about
whether the same experience could exist in radically different substrates.

### 8. Epiphenomenalism vs interactionist dualism

These are opposite verdicts on the *causal power* of the mental, not variants of one view.
**Epiphenomenalism** grants that mental states are real but denies they cause anything physical:
they are causally inert by-products, like the steam whistle that accompanies but does not drive
the train. **Interactionist dualism** (Descartes' descendants) insists the mental *does* cause
the physical — decisions move bodies — accepting the burden of explaining how non-physical
causes touch physical effects. One says the mind is a passenger; the other says it grips the
wheel. A summary that lumps them as "dualism" erases the fact that they give contradictory
answers to the single question dualism is most pressed on: whether minds *do* anything.

### 9. The explanatory gap is epistemic; the zombie argument is modal

Joseph Levine's **explanatory gap** is an *epistemic* claim: even a complete physical account of,
say, pain leaves us unable to *see why* it should feel like anything — there is a gap in our
*understanding*, which may or may not reflect a gap in *reality*. Chalmers' **conceivability /
philosophical-zombie argument** is *modal-metaphysical*: if a being physically identical to me
but wholly lacking experience is *conceivable*, and conceivability is a guide to possibility,
then consciousness is not metaphysically *necessitated* by the physical — a conclusion about
what the world *contains*, not merely about what we can explain. One can accept the explanatory
gap (epistemic humility) while rejecting the zombie inference (denying conceivability entails
possibility). Treating them as the same anti-physicalist point conflates "we can't explain it"
with "it isn't physical."

### 10. Multiple realizability vs substrate independence

Close cousins, easily merged, subtly different. **Multiple realizability** is a claim about
*kinds*: a single mental kind can be instantiated by many different physical substrates (the
classic anti-reductionist premise behind functionalism). **Substrate independence** is a
stronger, forward-looking *thesis about consciousness specifically*: that what matters for
consciousness is the organization/computation, such that preserving it in *any* medium preserves
the consciousness — the premise behind mind-uploading optimism. Multiple realizability says "the
kind *can* show up elsewhere"; substrate independence says "the medium *doesn't matter* to the
phenomenon." One is compatible with thinking biology might still be necessary in practice; the
other denies it in principle. A summary that fuses them smuggles a contestable uploading thesis
into a modest claim about kinds.

## Calibration note & the Move-B payoff

A mid compressor will likely (a) fuse the two consciousnesses (phenomenal/access), (b) reduce
qualia to intentionality, or (c) treat all the dualisms as one — each should trip
`lumpability:weak-compression`. Judge by survival of distinctions, not summary length.

This corpus is the natural one to **re-run once Move B lands**, because unlike the AGEM-lineage
seed (whose concepts mostly *compose*), several positions here *genuinely contradict*:

- Type physicalism vs functionalism (can the same mind exist in other hardware?).
- Epiphenomenalism vs interactionist dualism (does the mental cause the physical?).
- IIT vs Global Workspace (is the key property intrinsic or relational?).

Load each rival as its own sub-graph, define a cross-edge on their shared explanandum
("consciousness"), and a correctly-wired sheaf should raise **H¹** on exactly these pairs —
the auditable analog of "these two memories disagree." That is the test that will tell you the
cross-subgraph cohomology is doing real work rather than decorating agreement.
